# The Fourfold Openness Governance Framework

**Version 0.1 · 2026**  
**Public Draft for Discussion and Pilot Testing**

The Fourfold Openness Framework describes how lived experience may enter the common world without losing agency, meaning, cultural context, or the right to correction.

Openness is not automatic disclosure.  
Each form of openness requires a distinct threshold, decision process, and form of accountability.

---

## 1. Experience Openness

### Core Question

**What happened?**

### What May Be Opened

- field notes
- oral histories
- photographs
- audio and video recordings
- observations
- local knowledge
- records of everyday life

### Required Threshold

**Consent**

No experience should be opened without meaningful consent from the people whose lives, words, images, or knowledge are involved.

### Rights That Remain

Contributors may:

- limit the audience
- restrict particular uses
- request anonymity
- revise information
- withdraw permission for future use
- refuse AI processing

---

## 2. Inner Openness

### Core Question

**What does this experience mean to the person?**

### What May Be Opened

- feelings
- memories
- interpretations
- personal meanings
- reflections
- voluntary self-expression

### Required Threshold

**Legitimacy**

Expression must be voluntary, contextual, and respectful of the person's dignity.

A person may choose silence, ambiguity, anonymity, or partial disclosure.

### Rights That Remain

No system should treat emotional expression as permission for unrestricted analysis, diagnosis, prediction, generation, or training.

---

## 3. Cultural Openness

### Core Question

**How may many experiences contribute to shared knowledge and creation?**

### What May Be Opened

- learning
- citation
- translation
- comparison
- research
- interpretation
- co-creation
- cultural exchange

### Required Threshold

**Community Legitimacy**

Cultural use must preserve provenance, context, contribution relationships, and the right of affected people or communities to correct misrepresentation.

### Responsibilities

- sources remain traceable
- context remains attached
- interpretation remains contestable
- contributors receive attribution
- value returns to the source
- communities are not treated as raw material

---

## 4. Civilizational Openness

### Core Question

**How can different worlds collaborate without erasing difference?**

### What May Be Opened

- self-explanation
- bidirectional translation
- negotiation
- correction of misreading
- cross-cultural collaboration
- shared rule-making
- co-creation of future possibilities

### Required Threshold

**Translation and Correction**

No culture should be reduced to a single external description.

Different cultural worlds require continuing translation, mutual correction, and the ability to disagree.

### Responsibilities

- interpretation must remain revisable
- no single actor represents an entire civilization
- disagreement is legitimate
- shared rules require plural participation
- collaboration must not require cultural uniformity

---

## Core Principles

The framework is governed throughout by five principles:

1. **Consent** — Openness begins with meaningful consent.
2. **Provenance** — Source remains attached to content.
3. **Context** — Meaning must not be separated from its circumstances.
4. **Control** — Permissions may be limited, revised, withdrawn, or refused.
5. **Reciprocity** — Recognition and value should return to the source.

---

## Relationship Between the Four Forms

Experience provides the material.

Inner life gives experience meaning.

Culture forms shared patterns of knowledge and creation.

Civilizational openness creates interfaces through which different cultural worlds may collaborate.

These are not stages of superiority.

They are four different relationships through which experience may move from the personal toward wider forms of public and cross-cultural participation.
